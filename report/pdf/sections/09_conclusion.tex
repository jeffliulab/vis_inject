\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We composed two existing papers --- a multi-prompt PGD universal attack and a CLIP+Decoder fusion module --- into a three-stage pipeline that turns one universal adversarial image into many perceptually invisible adversarial photos at $\Linf = 16/255$ and PSNR $\approx 25.2$~dB. We evaluated $6{,}615$ (clean, adversarial) response pairs against four open \vlm{}s along two independent axes. The headline finding is that broad disruption ($\approx 66\%$) coexists with rare payload delivery ($0.227\%$), and that the few injections that do land cluster on screenshot-like images whose semantic class matches the payload's category. A manual transfer test against GPT-4o fails. BLIP-2 is fully immune across every test we ran. As a side-effect of writing the report carefully, the dataset has been picked up by ${\sim}300$ external downloads in its first month --- a small but real signal that the artefacts are useful beyond the classroom. The natural next step, sketched in \S\ref{sec:future}, is a five-attack / three-defence / two-frontier-model matrix that would let us replace the single anecdote about transferability with a measured number.
